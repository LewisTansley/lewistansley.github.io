Asset libraries do not mean asset flips, they just need to be used correctly.

My name is Lewis Tansley, and since 2009, I've been learning games development, something which I was deeply interested in from a young age. During much of this time, I have honed my skills in environment and level design - working with unreal engine 4 since late 2014 / early 2015.

Whilst learning and tailoring my skills, I have seen various instances of incredible scene work from other developers and designers using a mixture of pre-made assets and bespoke assets, all of which have expanded my interest into this field.

There are many immediate benefits to using asset libraries within your work, even the largest AAA studios use them. This is a result of a few key reasons. A studio, developer, creator or amateur may choose to use asset libraries as it aids the speeding up of development / design, prototyping of scenes amongst many others.

Where asset libraries shine - The Rules.

From my experience, and my knowledge of others' work, every instance in which asset libraries work brilliantly in favour of the designer and the scenes themselves is down to just a few rules. However obvious these may seem to some designers, I still regularly see ill - uses of libraries when these rules aren't upheld.

  • They are used sparingly: Not every model in a scene should just be pulled from an asset library, this results in less creative freedom and restricts what you, as a designer, are able to do.
  • They are used in conjunction with bespoke assets, or even modified posthumously to bring it in-line with either the art style or quality level of the scene, this allows both the acceleration of design, with the quality and art-direction of a fully bespoke scene.
  • They are selected to fit the scene: All models used from asset libraries must fit the general quality level of the scene, this means making sure texture quality remains harmonious across all models within the scene. Texel density must remain consistent within the scene and the chosen models themselves, if you put 2 objects together and one is less distinct this will appear obvious to the viewer, especially in a photorealistic space, simply breaking the illusion. The final and key portion of this is polycount, if there is an entire photorealistic scene and then one lower / low poly model sitting in it, that will draw the focus of the viewer and, once again, will break the immersion.
  • They accent key parts of a scene. Primarily meaning many small assets that make up the detail work of a scene, such as in the (unfinished) scene shown below that used many small assets like candles from asset packs to add more detail and life to a scene.

As long as these rules are followed the ability to make incredibly convincing scenes in much shorter time is possible, with the use of both bespoke and pre-made assets.
